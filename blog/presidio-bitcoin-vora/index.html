Self-Custody AI and Bitcoin

March 2026 · Vora

In October 2025, Erik Cason and Jesse Posner sat down with Presidio Bitcoin for an hour-long conversation about a question that should make every Bitcoiner uncomfortable: if we spent seventeen years learning how to hold our own money, what happens when the thing we need to hold is not money but intelligence?

The conversation is the most complete single-sitting articulation of the Vora thesis. What follows is the argument it makes and why the argument has gotten sharper, not softer, in the four months since the microphones turned off.

"Self-Custody AI and Bitcoin with Vora" — Presidio Bitcoin, October 20, 2025. Guests: Erik Cason & Jesse Posner.

The central argument is that self-custody is not a Bitcoin feature. It is a structural principle about control that Bitcoin happened to demonstrate first. When Antonopoulos said "not your keys, not your coins," he was making a specific claim about money: if you do not hold the cryptographic keys, you do not own the value. Cason and Posner extend the claim. If you do not hold the keys to your AI — the model, the memory, the hardware — you do not own your intelligence. And intelligence, in 2026, is the asset that directs everything else: your financial decisions, your health decisions, your legal reasoning, the way you make sense of the world at two in the morning when you cannot sleep and the questions are not small.

Posner's contribution to the conversation is the hardware layer, and it comes from a specific place. He spent years as senior architect of Coinbase's security system, protecting tens of billions in digital assets, then as a senior architect on the Bitkey project. His argument is precise: sovereignty that starts at the software layer is incomplete. If the hardware includes a proprietary secure element you cannot audit — a black box chip whose internals are trade secrets — the entire stack above it is a castle built on a foundation you cannot inspect. Vora's approach starts at the physical layer: FPGA-based security instead of opaque secure elements, open-source firmware, reproducible builds. If you cannot verify the silicon, the software running on it is a promise, not a proof. Posner has spent a decade in an industry where a single unverified assumption can mean irreversible loss of billions. He builds accordingly.

Cason's contribution is the philosophical frame. His NYKNYM thesis — Not Your Keys, Not Your Mind — names what is at stake. Cognition is upstream of everything. Upstream of the money, upstream of the decisions the money funds, upstream of the identity the decisions create. If the AI that helps you think is custodial — running on someone else's server, subject to someone else's alignment, sending telemetry to someone else's database — then the most important cognitive process in your life is controlled by an entity whose incentives are not yours. The entity does not need to be malicious. It only needs to be optimizing for something other than your sovereignty, and it is, because its business model requires it.

The design principle that follows is fiduciary AI. Not "we take your privacy seriously" — a sentence that appears in every corporate privacy policy, usually adjacent to the paragraph explaining how they share your data with "trusted partners." Fiduciary AI means the architecture enforces loyalty the way a proof enforces truth: not by promise but by structure. The AI runs on your hardware. The keys are yours. The memory is encrypted under your control. There is no server to subpoena, no database to breach, no business model that treats your inner life as training data waiting to be monetized. The loyalty is mathematical, not contractual. The difference between a privacy policy and a proof is that one of them survives contact with a company's next quarterly earnings call.

The podcast aired in October 2025. It is now March 2026. Five months. In AI, five months is a geological epoch. Everything Cason and Posner discussed has accelerated, and not in the direction of comfort.

OpenAI released open-weight models under Apache 2.0. The cathedral validating the bazaar's thesis. But the conversation anticipated a subtlety the open-weight enthusiasts continue to miss: open weights on someone else's server are still custodial. The weights are free. The inference is not yours. The model is open. The context window is theirs. The license is not the same as the custody, and confusing one for the other is the kind of mistake that sounds sophisticated until the server goes down and your agent's memory goes with it.

OpenClaw exploded — 220,000 GitHub stars in eighty-four days, proving that self-hosted AI agents are mainstream demand, not niche interest. And then the Bitcoin ban proved that even "sovereign" open-source projects can be captured by a single person's decisions, which is what happened when the creator banned Bitcoin from the Discord and then joined OpenAI. The sovereignty was real until it wasn't. This is what Cason and Posner mean when they say that sovereignty must be architectural, not personal. If the sovereignty depends on the continued good decisions of one individual, it is not sovereignty. It is benevolence with a shelf life.

Lightning Labs released L402 agent tools on February 11, 2026. The payment rail for sovereign AI agents is open-source and shipping. HTTP-native micropayments. Machine-to-machine invoicing. The economic layer of the sovereignty stack that Cason and Posner described as a goal is now infrastructure that developers are building on.

And the NYKNYM presentation happened. Plan B Forum, El Salvador, January 2026. The philosophical framework discussed in the Presidio conversation became a thirty-five-slide public argument delivered to the Bitcoin community. The seed that was planted in the October conversation became a thesis with a name, a structure, and an audience.

The podcast was the seed. The presentation was the flower.

The Presidio episode sits within a broader series of conversations that build the argument from different angles. On What Bitcoin Did, the case for Bitcoin as sovereign infrastructure. On THE Bitcoin Podcast, the physical security layer — securing your body and your keys for a world where the stakes are higher than money. On New Foundations, the practical threat model: wrench attacks, physical coercion, the unglamorous realities of self-custody that the philosophical essays prefer to skip. On Pleb Underground, the most recent, introducing Vora to the grassroots Bitcoin community that will be its first users.

The Presidio episode is where the layers converge. If you have read the NYKNYM thesis and want to hear the founders think through the same ideas in real time, with the digressions and qualifications that an essay smooths over, this is the conversation. If you are a Bitcoiner wondering why AI sovereignty should keep you up at night, or an AI developer wondering why Bitcoin keeps appearing in your agent's transaction logs, start here.

Vora exists because the thing that decides what the money is for is more valuable than the money. And that thing — the mind — needs custody as badly as the coins ever did.
