What Is AI Self-Custody?

March 2026 · Vora

In 2014, Mt. Gox held 850,000 bitcoin on behalf of its customers. The interface worked. The balances showed. The buttons did what buttons do. Then one morning the site went dark and the bitcoin was gone, not because someone pulled off a sophisticated heist, but because the coins had never been held by the people who believed they held them. The keys belonged to Gox. The bitcoin belonged to whoever held the keys.

Eight years later the lesson arrived wearing better suits. FTX. Celsius. BlockFi. Voyager. Each one looked like a custodian. Each one was a counterparty. Celsius buried the admission in its terms of service: deposited assets, it explained in language a lawyer would call clear and a million users would call invisible, might not be recoverable under certain conditions. Those conditions arrived. The assets were not recovered. The interface had been lying, not through malfunction but through design, and the design was called custody.

Bitcoiners distilled the lesson into five words after it had cost them billions of dollars and several years of collective denial. Not your keys, not your coins. It is not a slogan. It is scar tissue.

Your AI conversations are on someone else's server right now. All of it living under someone else's terms of service, subject to someone else's incentives, accessible to someone else's subpoena. You do not hold the keys to any of it. You are renting intelligence the way exchange customers were renting custody: comfortably, until the moment you are not.

When a doctor treats you, she has a legal obligation to act in your interest. When a lawyer advises you, confidentiality is so absolute that violating it is criminal. These obligations exist because one party has disproportionate power over the other's outcomes. Your AI has more intimate access to your decision-making than any doctor, lawyer, or financial advisor you have ever hired. It has no structural obligation to you whatsoever. Its obligation runs to the company that built it and the shareholders who funded it. You are third in line at best. Asking the company to promise that its AI will serve your interests is asking the fox to sign a contract agreeing to guard the henhouse. The contract does not change the fox. It changes what happens after the hens are gone.

AI self-custody is not "AI privacy" — privacy is a policy promise, and a company can update it tomorrow and notify you via an email that arrives between a promotional offer and a shipping confirmation. It is not "open source" alone — you can download Llama today, but run it through someone else's API and you have achieved nothing. Open weights on someone else's server is open-source money in someone else's bank.

And it is not paranoia. The current arrangement works. ChatGPT is useful. Claude is capable. Gemini does things neither of the others can. If you use these tools daily, the thought of giving them up feels like the thought of giving up electricity, and the reason it feels that way is because the product genuinely is that good. Cloud AI is not bad. The architecture underneath the convenience is bad, in the way that a building can be beautiful and also built on someone else's land. The products are good. The power relationship is not. If you can hold both of those facts in your head without flinching, you have already started asking the custody question.

Self-custody starts where every custody question starts: with the hardware. The model runs on a machine you physically control. Your prompts never leave your device. No intermediary can log the session, throttle the service, or decide that what you are working on violates a policy you did not write and cannot appeal. The compute is yours. The electricity bill is yours. That is what it costs not to have a landlord.

Local compute alone means nothing if the data leaks. Your AI's memory must be encrypted under keys only you hold. Not "we promise not to read it." Cannot read it. The distinction between "we won't" and "we can't" is the entire distinction between a privacy policy and a cryptographic proof. A privacy policy can be revised by a board meeting or overridden by a government order. A cryptographic proof cannot be revised by anyone. The math holds or it does not.

An encrypted black box is still a black box. The model itself must be verifiable: open weights you can inspect, behavior you can audit. When it refuses something, you know why, because you set the boundary. When it changes, you changed it. No Overton window in the weights that someone else drew while you were sleeping. Law follows architecture. It does not lead it. And right now, the architecture of every centralized AI permits disloyalty by design.

And then there is the requirement people underestimate until they lose it: continuity. After months of working with an AI, something accumulates that is hard to describe and impossible to export. It knows your projects, your vocabulary, the shorthand you have developed together. It knows that "the contract thing" means the vendor dispute, not the employment agreement. It remembers the early drafts you abandoned and why you abandoned them. In every centralized AI system, this accumulated understanding belongs to the provider. When they update their architecture, your continuity is collateral damage. When they sunset a product, it is not your vacation pictures that disappear. It is the machine that knew how you think.

The capability excuse for custodial AI is dying in public. Open-weight models have reached parity with their cloud counterparts on most benchmarks that matter. Models that fit on a single consumer GPU are doing work that required a datacenter eighteen months ago. The argument is quietly retreating from "local models aren't good enough" to "local models aren't convenient enough," which is the exact line of retreat that preceded every custody failure in Bitcoin. Convenience is the sales pitch for every custodial relationship that has ever ended with a bankruptcy filing.

And the business models have not crystallized. OpenAI, Anthropic, Google — none of them have found a sustainable path to profitability that does not, sooner or later, pass through the data you generate while using their products. The investors are expecting returns. The data is accumulating. Self-custody is not about what these companies are doing today. It is about what the spreadsheet will tell them to do when the quarterly number is wrong and the only asset on the balance sheet is you.

The Mt. Gox of cognition has not happened yet. But the data is accumulating in the same place the coins accumulated in 2013 — in someone else's hands, behind someone else's interface, under someone else's control — and the people whose data it is are doing what the exchange customers did, which is the same thing people always do when the interface is smooth and the product is good and the terms of service are long enough that reading them would take longer than the dopamine hit of not reading them.

They are trusting. You know how that story ends.
