What Is AI Self-Custody?

March 2026 · Vora

In 2014, Mt. Gox held 850,000 bitcoin on behalf of its customers. The interface worked. The balances showed. The buttons did what buttons do. Then one morning the site went dark and the bitcoin was gone, not because someone pulled off a sophisticated heist, but because the coins had never been held by the people who believed they held them. The keys belonged to Gox. The bitcoin belonged to whoever held the keys.

Eight years later the lesson arrived wearing better suits. FTX. Celsius. BlockFi. Voyager. Each one looked like a custodian. Each one was a counterparty. Celsius buried the admission in its terms of service: deposited assets, it explained in language a lawyer would call clear and a million users would call invisible, might not be recoverable under certain conditions. Those conditions arrived. The assets were not recovered. The interface had been lying, not through malfunction but through design, and the design was called custody.

Bitcoiners distilled the lesson into five words after it had cost them billions of dollars and several years of collective denial. Not your keys, not your coins. It is not a slogan. It is scar tissue.

Your AI conversations are on someone else's server right now. Your cognitive patterns, the rough drafts you abandoned, the midnight questions you have never asked another human being, the reasoning process that constitutes how you actually make up your mind. All of it living under someone else's terms of service, subject to someone else's incentives, accessible to someone else's subpoena. You do not hold the keys to any of it. You are renting intelligence the way exchange customers were renting custody: comfortably, until the moment you are not.

AI self-custody is not "AI privacy" — privacy is a policy promise, and a company can update it tomorrow and notify you via an email that arrives between a promotional offer and a shipping confirmation. It is not "open source" alone — you can download Llama today, but run it through someone else's API and you have achieved nothing. Open weights on someone else's server is open-source money in someone else's bank.

And it is not paranoia. I want to be honest about this because the rest of the argument depends on it. The current arrangement works. ChatGPT is useful. Claude is capable. Gemini does things neither of the others can. If you use these tools daily, the thought of giving them up feels like the thought of giving up electricity, and the reason it feels that way is because the product genuinely is that good. Cloud AI is not bad. The architecture underneath the convenience is bad, in the way that a building can be beautiful and also built on someone else's land. The products are good. The power relationship is not. If you can hold both of those facts in your head without flinching, you have already started asking the custody question.

Self-custody starts where every custody question starts: with the hardware. The model runs on a machine you physically control. Your prompts never leave your device. The sequence of questions and follow-ups and corrections that constitutes your reasoning process does not traverse a network you do not own. No intermediary can log the session, throttle the service, or decide that what you are working on violates a policy you did not write and cannot appeal. The compute is yours. The electricity bill is yours. That is what it costs not to have a landlord.

Local compute alone means nothing if the data leaks. Your AI's memory, the conversation history and accumulated context that makes it useful rather than merely capable, must be encrypted under keys only you hold. Not a third party offering to "manage your keys for convenience," which is a phrase that has preceded every major custody failure in the history of digital assets, spoken each time with the same reassuring smile and the same catastrophic result.

An encrypted black box is still a black box. The model itself must be verifiable: open weights you can inspect, behavior you can audit. When it refuses something, you know why, because you set the boundary. When it changes, you changed it. No opaque API whose responses shift because a company shipped an alignment update on a Tuesday afternoon that nobody outside the building voted on. No Overton window in the weights that someone else drew while you were sleeping.

And then there is the requirement people underestimate until they lose it: continuity. After months of working with an AI, something accumulates that is hard to describe and impossible to export. It knows your projects, your vocabulary, the shorthand you have developed together. It knows that "the contract thing" means the vendor dispute, not the employment agreement. It has read the early drafts you later abandoned and remembers why you abandoned them. It remembers the conversation you had at 1 AM when you were trying to figure out whether to quit your job, and it remembers what you decided, and it remembers what you decided before that, and the difference between the two is the most valuable thing any system has ever known about you. In every centralized AI system, this accumulated understanding belongs to the provider. When they update their architecture, your continuity is collateral damage. When they sunset a product, your cognitive context evaporates, the same way your photos went with every social network you ever quit, except this time it is not your vacation pictures that disappear. It is the machine that knew how you think.

The capability excuse for custodial AI is dying in public. Open-weight models have reached parity with their cloud counterparts on most benchmarks that matter. Models that fit on a single consumer GPU are doing work that required a datacenter eighteen months ago. The argument is quietly retreating from "local models aren't good enough" to "local models aren't convenient enough," which is the exact line of retreat that preceded every custody failure in Bitcoin. Your own wallet is not convenient enough. True. Also irrelevant. Convenience is the sales pitch for every custodial relationship that has ever ended with a bankruptcy filing.

Meanwhile, AI is becoming the first witness to your inner life. Search engines saw what you wanted to find. Social media saw what you wanted others to see. AI sees the process by which you make up your mind — your doubts before they resolve, your plans before they are ready, the half-formed heresies you test in the safety of a conversation you believe is private, the question you asked at 2 AM that you would never ask your spouse or your therapist or your priest. Not your keys, not your mind. The intimacy of this data is not incrementally greater than what came before. It is categorically different, the way a diary is categorically different from a shopping list.

And the business models have not crystallized. OpenAI, Anthropic, Google — none of them have found a sustainable path to profitability that does not, sooner or later, pass through the data you generate while using their products. The investors are expecting returns. The data is accumulating. Somewhere in San Francisco right now, someone is building a slide deck that includes the phrase "monetize cognitive data" or some euphemism elegant enough to survive a congressional hearing, and the dataset they are describing is the one you built last night when you asked an AI to help you think through whether your marriage is working. Self-custody is not about what these companies are doing today. It is about what the spreadsheet will tell them to do when the quarterly number is wrong and the only asset on the balance sheet is you.

Vora is building the ownership layer for intelligence the way Bitcoin built the ownership layer for value. Local execution, key-based encryption, verifiable behavior, cognitive custody enforced by architecture. The whole stack, held by you. Not because the cloud products are bad — they are the best tools most people have ever used — but because the best tool in the world is still someone else's tool, and the history of someone else's tool is the history of terms that change, features that disappear, and data that ends up somewhere its creator never intended and never approved.

The Mt. Gox of cognition has not happened yet. But the data is accumulating in the same place the coins accumulated in 2013 — in someone else's hands, behind someone else's interface, under someone else's control — and the people whose data it is are doing what the exchange customers did, which is the same thing people always do when the interface is smooth and the product is good and the terms of service are long enough that reading them would take longer than the dopamine hit of not reading them.

They are trusting. You know how that story ends.
