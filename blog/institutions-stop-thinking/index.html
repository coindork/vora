When Institutions Stop Thinking

May 2026 · Vora

The lawyer notices it on the third page of the sentencing recommendation. A case citation she has never seen — State v. Henderson, 2019, a precedent that would be directly relevant if it existed. She checks. It does not exist. The case was invented by the AI system the court adopted eighteen months ago to improve efficiency in pre-sentencing reports. The system was producing recommendations at four times the speed of the overworked clerks it replaced. It was also, occasionally, fabricating the legal reasoning those recommendations were built on.

The judge who reviewed the recommendation before the hearing did not catch the hallucinated citation. This is not a surprising failure. It is the predictable result of a judge whose job has become reviewing outputs rather than producing judgments, and who reviews with the attention human beings bring to any task they have been quietly told is someone else's responsibility.

This is one courtroom in one county. The pattern is in every institution you trust.

You walk into a hospital and the diagnosis was generated before the doctor entered the room. You stand before a court and the sentencing recommendation was drafted by a system the judge cannot audit. You read a newspaper and the article was written by a model, then lightly touched by a human whose job title still says journalist. The institution looks the same — the building, the white coat, the black robe, the masthead, every signal of authority intact. The thing the institution was designed to do — deliberate, reason, weigh evidence, exercise judgment — has been outsourced to a system that does none of those things.

Fifty-eight percent of organizations now have AI embedded in enterprise-wide strategies, according to Info-Tech's 2026 survey. Only nineteen percent have implemented governance frameworks for that AI. Four out of five institutions running AI have not built any structure for overseeing what the AI does with the cognitive processes they handed it. This is not a planning failure. It is incentives working as designed. Institutions answer to stakeholders who measure output. Judgment is invisible in a spreadsheet. Speed is visible. Volume is visible. And in the absence of a column for "quality of reasoning," reasoning becomes the first thing to go.

The legal system is where the hollowing is most visible, because law is where judgment is most explicitly the point.

The adversarial process is not a formality. It is the mechanism by which law produces justice rather than procedure. Two sides, each motivated to find flaws in the other's argument, submit their strongest case to a judge obligated to weigh them and produce a decision the judge owns. The ownership matters. A judge who owns a ruling can be appealed. The reasoning can be examined against precedent, statute, constitutional principle by other humans who have also done the work of thinking. Accountability runs on ownership. Remove the ownership, and the accountability is theater.

A Colombian judge used ChatGPT to draft a ruling in 2023. The practice has spread, not retreated, in the years since — because why would it retreat? The efficiency gains are real, and nobody in the system is incentivized to measure what is lost. Legal research firms are deploying AI that hallucinates case citations, inventing precedents that sound authoritative in opinions citing decisions that were never decided. Sentencing models train on historically biased data and produce recommendations that encode the prejudices of the past into the judgments of the present while wearing the mask of algorithmic objectivity.

The adversarial process is being replaced by a generation pipeline, and the humans in the room are performing review — which is quality control applied to someone else's work. Quality control is not judgment, no matter how earnestly the person performing it believes otherwise. Law without judgment is bureaucracy. Bureaucracy with the power of law — the power to sentence, to confine, to take your children — is something darker than either, and it is arriving in courtrooms right now, wearing a robe.

Education has the same disease with a longer incubation period. A university exists to do one thing no other institution does: take a person who receives information and develop them into a person who can evaluate it, challenge it, produce something original from the encounter. Information transfer has been free since the printing press. The university's irreplaceable function is transformation, not transfer.

When students submit AI-generated work demonstrating competencies they do not possess, the obvious crisis is the student. The institutional crisis is worse. When professors outsource curriculum design, the university no longer decides what it teaches. When assessment is AI-generated, the university no longer evaluates what it certifies. When research runs through summarization rather than primary engagement with sources, the university no longer produces knowledge — it produces outputs that pass the formal tests for knowledge while empty of the thing that makes knowledge different from information: a human mind that struggled with material until it yielded. The commencement speaker still says "excellence." You are paying two hundred thousand dollars for a certificate from a factory that has forgotten what it was supposed to certify.

Journalism is further along the curve. The euphemism "AI-assisted" covers a spectrum from "the AI drafted and a human rewrote" to "the AI wrote everything and a human changed three words and attached a byline," and the spectrum is collapsing toward the second pole. Investigation has been replaced by search. Analysis by summarization. The newsroom retains its masthead, and the masthead no longer represents what it was supposed to: a group of humans who encountered reality on its own terms, struggled with what they found, and produced an account bearing the marks of that struggle. You cannot hold a model accountable for a story. You cannot evaluate whether a model exercised courage in deciding what to publish. The struggle was the journalism. The rest is typesetting.

I should say what is hard to say, because the institutional sovereignty argument has a dishonest version too. Some of these institutions deserved to be disrupted. The doctor who spent three minutes with you before prescribing was not exercising deep judgment. The judge processing two hundred cases a week was already rubber-stamping before AI made it efficient. The journalist writing listicles at volume was already a content factory before the model arrived. In many cases, AI is replacing a process that was already degraded, and the people mourning institutional judgment are mourning an ideal that the institution had abandoned long before the machine showed up.

That admission does not change the structural problem. Even degraded judgment is still judgment — a human in the room who can be questioned, held accountable, who carries experience and doubt and the capacity to notice when something is wrong in a way that matches no pattern in the training data. Replace that human with a model and you do not get better judgment. You get no judgment. You get outputs at scale, with the institutional authority still attached, and nobody in the room who owns the decision when the output is wrong. The degradation of judgment was a problem. The elimination of judgment is a catastrophe. And the catastrophe looks, from the outside, like an improvement, because the outputs are faster, the wait times shorter, and the gap between the institution's incentives and your interests is papered over by efficiency metrics nobody asked you to approve.

Cognitive sovereignty for individuals is necessary. It is not sufficient. You can hold your own keys, run your own model, think your own thoughts with perfect autonomy. Then you walk into a hospital that does not think, stand before a court that does not think, send your child to a school that does not think. Individual sovereignty in a civilization of hollowed institutions is sovereignty in a vacuum.

The honest thing to say is that I do not know what institutional cognitive sovereignty looks like at scale. I know what it looks like for a person — hold the model, hold the keys, hold the context. I know the principle extends: an institution that runs its own models, maintains its own deliberative capacity, refuses to outsource the judgment that is its reason for existing. But the gap between principle and implementation is vast, and pretending otherwise is the kind of clean answer this essay has been arguing against. The hardest question is the institutional one, and I am not going to pretend I have solved it by naming it.

What I can say is that the caste system forming between individuals who hold their own cognition and individuals who do not has an institutional counterpart. Institutions that maintain genuine deliberative capacity will produce different outcomes than institutions that generate outputs from models they do not control. The gap will compound. And the people who depend on the hollowed institutions — which is most people, because most people do not choose their hospital, their school district, their local court — will bear the cost of a surrender they did not consent to.

The authority is still there. The building is still there. The robe, the white coat, the masthead, the diploma — all still there. The thinking is gone, and nobody told you, because the institution has no incentive to tell you, and the system that replaced the thinking was specifically selected because it produces outputs indistinguishable from judgment at a fraction of the cost.

The question nobody asks — who is actually thinking? — is the question on which everything depends. Not who has access to AI. Not who governs AI. Who is doing the thinking that institutions were built to protect? If the answer, when you trace it to the bottom, is "a model that cannot be held accountable, trained on data it did not choose, optimized for objectives it cannot explain" — then the authority is a costume. And you are standing in front of it, trusting the costume, because nobody told you the person wearing it left the building months ago.
