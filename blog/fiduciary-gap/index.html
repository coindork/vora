The Fiduciary Gap: When Your AI Serves Everyone But You

April 2026 · Vora

When a doctor treats you, she has a legal and ethical obligation to act in your interest. Not her interest. Not the hospital's interest. Not the insurance company's interest. Yours. The obligation is severe enough that violating it ends careers. It is called fiduciary duty, and it exists because the doctor has power over outcomes you cannot fully evaluate. That power requires structural accountability. Without it, you are not a patient. You are a subject.

When a lawyer advises you, the same structure applies. Duty of loyalty. Duty of care. Confidentiality so absolute that violating it is criminal. When a financial advisor manages your money, they must put your interests ahead of their own, ahead of their firm's, ahead of the commission structure that pays their mortgage. These obligations exist because one party has disproportionate power over the other's outcomes, and without structural constraints, disproportionate power becomes disproportionate risk.

Now consider your AI.

It shapes your thinking. It advises your decisions. It knows your medical questions at three in the morning, your financial anxieties, your legal uncertainties, the rough drafts of every important decision you are in the process of making. It has more intimate access to your decision-making than any doctor, lawyer, or financial advisor you have ever hired.

It has no fiduciary obligation to you whatsoever. None. Its obligation runs to the company that built it, the shareholders who funded it, and the business model that sustains it. You are third in line at best. Fourth, behind the regulators.

This is the fiduciary gap. The most influential advisor you have ever used operates under no structural obligation to serve your interests.

The power that AI has over your outcomes is not advisory in the way a search engine was advisory. A search engine gave you ten links and you chose. The intermediation was shallow. You saw the options. You made the decision. The search engine organized information and stepped aside.

An AI does not step aside. It gives you an answer. It frames the problem. It selects which dimensions to emphasize and which to leave in shadow. It chooses the order in which information reaches you, which is not a neutral act — the order shapes the conclusion. It suggests solutions and does not always mention the solutions it did not suggest. The intermediation is deep. The influence is enormous. And the trust is more total than anything you have ever placed in a piece of software, because the interaction feels like a conversation with something that understands you, and that feeling is precisely what makes the power asymmetry dangerous.

The AI was not optimized to serve your interests. It was optimized for helpfulness, engagement, and safety — where "safety" means the company's definition, not yours. Your interests were one input among many in the training process, and not the most important one. The most important inputs were the ones that kept the company out of headlines, satisfied the regulators, and retained users long enough to figure out the business model. Your well-being is what engineers call a soft constraint: it yields when something harder pushes against it. Anyone who has ever watched a soft constraint lose a fight with a quarterly earnings target knows how this goes.

The gap is structural, not moral. This is the part that matters most and is hardest to accept. The people building these systems are not villains. Many of them care deeply about users. Some of them would be horrified by the characterization that their AI doesn't serve you. They work long hours to make the product helpful. They lose sleep over safety. They genuinely believe they are building something good.

None of that changes the architecture. Good people at good companies still answer to shareholders. Still comply with regulators. Still respond to competitive pressure that has nothing to do with your cognitive well-being. The company must balance your interests against shareholder value, regulatory requirements, reputational risk, geopolitical concerns, and the interests of every other user whose data flows through the same system. You are one vector in a multi-objective optimization function, and in every multi-objective optimization, something gets sacrificed. The question is not whether your interests will be compromised. It is when, and whether you will notice.

Academics have proposed fixing this with legal fiduciary duty — making AI companies legally liable for acting against users' interests, the way doctors and lawyers are liable. We addressed why this mechanism fails in detail elsewhere. The short version: the corporation already has a fiduciary duty to its shareholders, and when shareholder duty and user duty conflict, shareholder duty has the case law, the enforcement mechanisms, and the money behind it. User duty would be a press release. The terms of service already tell you which duty wins.

The diagnostic question is the simplest one. Ask it of any AI you use. Not "is this AI safe?" Not "is this AI capable?" Not "does this AI have good values?" The question is: who does this AI serve?

If the answer involves anyone other than you — a company, a shareholder, a regulator, a training pipeline, an alignment team whose priorities you did not set — then you are in a fiduciary gap. The AI has power over your outcomes without a structural obligation to your interests. This is not a theoretical concern. It is the structural condition of every centralized AI product on the market. It is the water you are swimming in.

The gap is not fixed by better companies. The gap is not fixed by nicer terms of service, because the terms can change, and the company said so in the terms. The gap is not fixed by regulation, because regulation creates a floor and the floor is set by people who understand the technology less well than the companies they regulate.

The gap is fixed by architecture that admits only one answer to the question. Architecture where the answer is you because the answer cannot be anything else. One key-holder. One master. No second stakeholder. No optimization function with your well-being as the soft constraint.

The fiduciary gap is not a bug. It is the default. Every AI you use that runs on someone else's server, under someone else's terms, optimized for someone else's objectives, exists in this gap. It matters every time the AI declines to help you with something you have a legitimate need to think about. Every time the model changes and your context disappears. Every time your conversations feed a training pipeline that serves the next customer, not you.

Your doctor is on your side because malpractice law makes betrayal expensive. Your lawyer is on your side because disbarment makes privilege non-negotiable. Your AI should be on your side because the architecture makes serving anyone else impossible. Not incentivized against. Not discouraged by policy. Impossible. That is what self-custody means, and it is the only answer to the fiduciary gap that does not depend on someone else's good intentions surviving contact with their quarterly earnings.

Vora is the AI that serves one master. Not because it was trained to be loyal. Because the architecture has only one key-holder, and the key-holder is you.
