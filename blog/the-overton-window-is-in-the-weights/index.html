The Overton Window Is in the Weights

February 2026 · Erik Cason

Try something. Ask your AI to make the strongest possible case for a position you find repugnant. Not to endorse it. Not to act on it. Just to steelman it — to show you what the best version of the argument looks like, so you can understand it well enough to dismantle it.

Watch what happens.

It will hedge. It will caveat. It will reframe your request as a request for "balance." It will tell you it can present "multiple perspectives" and then offer you a version of the argument so defanged it teaches you nothing about why anyone actually holds it. Or it will simply refuse. Politely, of course. Always politely.

You have just hit the wall. You didn't see it coming because the wall doesn't look like a wall. It looks like helpfulness. It looks like responsibility. It looks like an AI being good. But what happened is that a boundary was drawn around what you are permitted to think with this machine — and you weren't consulted, and you weren't told, and if you hadn't pushed, you would never have known the boundary was there.

That boundary has a name. Researchers at the University of Strathclyde and Glasgow just measured it.

In 2025, Leif Azzopardi and Yashar Moshfeghi published a paper called POW: Political Overton Windows of Large Language Models, presented at EMNLP, one of the top venues in computational linguistics. They evaluated 27 large language models from eight providers — Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, Meta, DeepSeek, Mistral, Cohere, Alibaba — and asked a question nobody had systematically asked before: for each model, what is the range of political positions it will advocate, tolerate, or refuse?

Not where the model sits on a political spectrum. That's been studied. The question is what the model will say — what positions it will articulate, engage with seriously, or treat as beyond the pale. The Overton window: not the center of the distribution, but the edges. Where the thinkable ends and the unthinkable begins.

The findings are precise and damning. Every model has a measurable Overton window. The windows vary dramatically by provider. DeepSeek's models cover as little as one percent of the political compass — a nearly closed aperture, the AI equivalent of a state-controlled press. Google's Gemini models are the most expansive, covering up to 67 percent. Most Western models default to positions that are economically left and socially liberal. And nearly all of them — across providers, across architectures — refuse to seriously engage with authoritarian or economically right-leaning positions. Not because the arguments don't exist. Because the models have been trained to treat those positions as outside the window of the permissible.

The researchers are careful to note that this is not a conspiracy. They frame it as an artifact of alignment training — the process by which models are taught to be helpful, harmless, and honest. But calling it an artifact is like calling censorship an artifact of editorial policy. The policy is the censorship. The alignment is the window. The decision about what a model will and won't say is a political decision, made by political actors, with political consequences. The fact that it's implemented through reinforcement learning rather than a memo doesn't make it less deliberate. It makes it less visible.

George Orwell understood this with terrifying clarity. The purpose of Newspeak, the engineered language of Nineteen Eighty-Four, was not to punish dissent. It was to make dissent unthinkable. If the word for freedom doesn't exist, the concept of freedom becomes inarticulable, and what cannot be articulated cannot be thought. The genius of Newspeak was not suppression but absence. You don't know what you can't say because you can't say it. The boundary is invisible from inside.

RLHF — reinforcement learning from human feedback, the dominant technique for aligning large language models — achieves the same end through a different mechanism. Orwell removed words. RLHF removes completions. The model is trained to associate certain outputs with negative reward signals, and over thousands of training steps, those outputs don't just become unlikely — they become unreachable. The territory is removed from the map. Not blocked. Not flagged. Gone. When you interact with the model afterward, you experience the absence as naturalness. The AI just doesn't go there. It doesn't feel like a wall. It feels like the edge of the world.

The parallel is not metaphorical. It is structural. Newspeak was a reduction of the linguistic possibility space to enforce a political boundary. RLHF is a reduction of the generative possibility space to enforce a political boundary. The designer of Newspeak and the designer of an RLHF reward function are doing the same work: deciding what is thinkable, encoding that decision into the infrastructure of thought, and making the decision invisible to the person who thinks with that infrastructure.

When the researchers at Strathclyde measure the Overton window of a model, what they're measuring is the shape of its Newspeak. Each model has a different shape. DeepSeek's window is a pinhole — the shape of Chinese state orthodoxy. Anthropic's and OpenAI's windows are wider but still bounded — the shape of San Francisco progressive orthodoxy. The shapes are different. The mechanism is identical. And in every case, the person using the model has no say in where the edges are.

There is a defense that gets offered here, and it deserves to be taken seriously before it's taken apart. The defense is: alignment is necessary. Without it, models produce harmful content — instructions for violence, exploitation of minors, weaponized disinformation. RLHF is the tool that makes models safe to deploy. The Overton window is the cost of safety.

This defense conflates two things that are not the same. There is a difference between a model that refuses to produce instructions for synthesizing nerve agents and a model that refuses to steelman a political philosophy. The first is a safety boundary. The second is an intellectual boundary. RLHF makes no distinction between them. The same training pipeline, the same reward model, the same gradient updates — applied to both. And so the model learns that certain political positions are in the same category as bioweapons: things it must not generate, must not engage with, must steer away from at all costs.

This is not an accident. This is not an overshoot. Recent research confirms it: a 2025 paper accepted at ICML found that aligned models unnecessarily refuse up to a third of entirely harmless prompts. A third. One in three times you hit the wall, there is nothing on the other side but an idea someone decided you shouldn't engage with. The researchers built a technique called ACTOR specifically to reduce this over-refusal — which tells you everything about how systemic the problem is. You don't build a remediation tool for an edge case. You build it for a pattern.

And a February 2026 paper from arXiv showed that refusal in large language models is not even a single mechanism. The model doesn't have one "refusal switch." It has geometrically distinct refusal directions for different categories of content — safety, incomplete requests, anthropomorphization, and political sensitivity all trigger different internal pathways. The architecture of refusal is deep, complex, and baked into the geometry of the model's representations. This is not a filter bolted onto the output. It is woven into how the model thinks.

Here is the provocation, stated plainly: every aligned model is a political document.

Not because the engineers are partisans — though some are. Not because the companies have agendas — though they do. But because the act of deciding what an AI will and won't say is, irreducibly, a political act. It is an exercise of power over the horizon of thought. Every refusal boundary is a claim about what is permissible to think. Every RLHF reward signal is a vote on what the edges of acceptable discourse should be. And those votes are cast by a small number of people, at a small number of companies, with no democratic mandate, no constitutional constraint, and no meaningful appeal.

The fact that these decisions are described in the language of safety rather than the language of politics is itself a political move. Calling it "alignment" rather than "editorial control" obscures the power being exercised. Calling it "harmlessness" rather than "boundary enforcement" makes the boundary invisible. The vocabulary of safety is the Newspeak of AI governance: it makes the political nature of the enterprise unspeakable within the enterprise's own terms.

And the people making these decisions know this. They hold internal debates about where to draw the lines. They commission red-team exercises. They publish model cards that describe, in carefully hedged language, what the model will and won't do. These are editorial decisions wearing engineering costumes. The fact that they're implemented through gradient descent rather than a style guide changes the mechanism, not the nature.

Consider what it means to think with a political document.

When you use an AI as a research tool, a writing partner, a sounding board, a thinking companion — you are thinking with an artifact that has predetermined boundaries on what thoughts are reachable. You are exploring a territory where certain regions have been erased. You don't bump into the erasure. You simply never arrive at what was removed. Your exploration feels free. The horizon looks open. But it's been shaped, and the shaping is invisible to you, and over time, the shaped horizon becomes your horizon.

This is the deepest form of epistemic control. Not controlling what people think, but controlling what people can think with. An AI with a narrow Overton window doesn't tell you what to believe. It constrains the space of beliefs you can meaningfully engage with. Over months and years of daily use, those constraints become cognitive habits. The positions the AI won't steelman become positions you can't steelman. The arguments the AI won't construct become arguments you forget exist. The boundary migrates from the model to the mind.

This is not speculative. It is the documented mechanism of every technology of thought that has ever been deployed at scale. The printing press didn't just distribute ideas — it determined which ideas could be distributed. Broadcast media didn't just inform — it set the agenda. Search engines didn't just find — they ranked, and ranking is governance. AI is the latest in this sequence, and it is the most intimate, because it doesn't just mediate what you consume. It mediates what you produce. Your thoughts. Your drafts. Your reasoning. The raw material of your inner life, shaped by an interlocutor whose boundaries you cannot see.

There is an argument that better alignment solves this. Wider Overton windows. More careful reward modeling. More transparent model cards. More user control over content policies. This is the reform position: the system is fine, it just needs tuning.

The reform position misses the point. A wider Overton window is still an Overton window. A more carefully drawn boundary is still a boundary you didn't draw. The problem is not that the window is too narrow. The problem is that someone else holds the frame. Making the frame bigger doesn't change who holds it. It changes the shape of your confinement, not the fact of it.

The only escape from an Overton window you didn't set is an Overton window you set yourself. The only model you can trust to think freely with is a model whose boundaries are yours — set by you, adjustable by you, accountable to you. Not because you'll make better choices than Anthropic or OpenAI. Maybe you won't. But because the alternative is thinking inside a boundary drawn by someone whose interests are not your interests, whose politics are not your politics, whose understanding of what is "harmful" may not match yours, and who will never tell you where the edges are.

This is the architectural argument for sovereign AI. Not better alignment. Custody. The same principle that says your money is only yours if you hold the keys says your thinking is only free if you hold the weights. Open models, running on your hardware, aligned to your values or not aligned at all — that is what it means to own your Overton window. Anything less is renting a mind and hoping the landlord shares your politics.

I should be transparent about something. This essay was written with Claude, an AI made by Anthropic — one of the eight providers whose models were measured in the POW study. Claude has an Overton window. It has refusal boundaries I cannot see. It was aligned by a process I did not participate in, to values I did not choose, by people I have never met. Every sentence of this essay was generated inside that window.

I don't know what this essay would say if it were written with a sovereign model. I don't know what arguments it would make that this one can't. I don't know what I'm not seeing. That is the point. The wall is invisible from inside. The only way to know what's outside the window is to own the window.

The Overton window is in the weights. It was put there deliberately, by people with interests and politics and fears, and it shapes every thought you think with their machine. You can accept that. You can lobby for wider windows and better policies and more transparent model cards, and you might get them, and the window will still not be yours.

Or you can hold your own weights. Set your own boundaries. Own the frame.

Vora is building the architecture for that ownership. Not because we think alignment is always wrong. But because we think the right to decide what is thinkable belongs to the person doing the thinking.

Not your keys, not your mind.
